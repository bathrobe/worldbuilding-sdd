# Question Sources

Reference material for `/questions`. Draw from these frameworks, adapted to the world's seed.

## Patricia Wrede's Fantasy Worldbuilding Questions (7 categories)

Adapt these — swap "magic" for whatever the world's equivalent system is.

1. **The World** — physical laws, geography, non-human inhabitants, continental layout, seasons, cosmology
2. **Physical & Historical Features** — natural resources, climate specifics, how history shaped current state, ruins, monuments, disasters
3. **Magic/Technology Systems** — what it can't do (more important than what it can), limits, costs, who certifies practitioners, political influence, mass production, failure modes
4. **Peoples & Customs** — rites of passage, beauty standards, greeting customs, how peoples view each other, trade languages, taboos, humor
5. **Social Organization** — government style, tax systems, succession, occupations respected/despised, crime & justice, forensic tech, citizen rights by class, how dissent works
6. **Commerce & Trade** — trade routes, economic power distribution, currency, industries, transportation, smuggling, debt, wealth display
7. **Daily Life** — fashion, manners, diet, education, calendar systems, sanitation, family structure, aging, death customs

## Brandon Sanderson's Laws

Not just for magic — apply to any core system (AI governance, post-scarcity economics, etc.):

- **First Law**: The audience's ability to believe in a resolution is proportional to how well they understand the system. Hard systems = clear constraints. Soft systems = mystery.
- **Second Law**: Limitations are more interesting than powers. What the system CAN'T do matters more than what it can. Costs, restrictions, failure modes.
- **Third Law**: Expand what you have, don't add new things. A few systems explored deeply > many systems sketched shallowly.

Frame questions around constraints: "What can't it do? What's the cost? What breaks when it fails? Who gets hurt by the limitation?"

## Real-World Frameworks

Pull from these based on what the seed suggests:

- **Economics**: Acemoglu (institutional economics, why nations fail), Brynjolfsson (AI economics, second machine age), Keynes (post-scarcity), Piketty (wealth concentration), Ostrom (commons governance)
- **Governance**: Audrey Tang & vTaiwan (digital democracy, radical transparency), Swiss cantons (federated governance), Venetian Republic (oligarchic republic), Hanseatic League (trade federation), Athenian democracy (direct participation + exclusion)
- **Ecology**: Getz's biomass transformation model (10 ecological roles), Odum (energy systems ecology), island biogeography, carrying capacity, trophic cascades
- **Anthropology**: Graeber (debt, bullshit jobs), Scott (seeing like a state, legibility), Dunbar numbers, kinship systems, gift economies
- **Science Fiction precedents**: Banks' Culture (post-scarcity), Palmer's Terra Ignota (federated nation-states), Le Guin's Dispossessed (anarchist society), Chiang (technology + humanity), Doctorow (digital rights), Robinson (ecology), Chambers (found family in space)

## Question Quality

Good questions are specific, provocative, sometimes zany. Not "What is the government like?" but:

- "What does a landlord-tenant dispute look like when the landlord is an AI?"
- "What's the 911-equivalent call in this system?"
- "If [social problem] is a public health crisis, what does the treatment ward look like?"
- "What does a Tuesday look like for a 35-year-old single parent in this system?"
- "What does a conspiracy theory look like in a world where the AI knows the truth?"
- "What's the worst thing about living here that the tourist brochure doesn't mention?"
- "When two [entities] disagree about resource allocation, who breaks the tie?"

Generate images for particularly visual questions when tools are configured.
